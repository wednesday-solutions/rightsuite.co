# GTM Validation Tools for SaaS Founders: What to Use for Each Decision

About 35% of SaaS startups fail because of poor product-market fit - but most of those failures happen at the GTM layer, not the product layer. The product exists. The audience, price, copy, and channel were never tested. There are 7 GTM decisions every SaaS founder has to make before launch, and each one has a specific validation approach that produces scored signal rather than opinion.

Here is what to use for each.

## The 7 GTM decisions and the right tool for each

### 1. Who to sell to

The audience decision determines every other GTM variable downstream. Get the segment wrong and your price is wrong, your copy is wrong, and your channel is wrong - all at the same time. Most founders pick a segment because it feels familiar, not because they have scored evidence that it has the highest purchase intent. The right validation approach is to name 2-3 candidate segments and rank them by urgency, willingness to pay, and objection pattern before choosing one.

[RightAudience]({{ '/docs/products/right-audience/' | relative_url }}) runs 100+ synthetic buyers per segment and returns a ranked list of your candidates by purchase intent score, willingness-to-pay range, and the top objections each segment raises. The segment that ranks first is your launch ICP.

### 2. How to differentiate

Your buyer is comparing you to something - a direct competitor, a workflow, or the option of doing nothing. Positioning that ignores those alternatives produces copy that feels generic to buyers even when it reads clearly to the founder. The validation you need is not "does my positioning make sense" but "does my positioning make me the obvious choice over the specific alternatives my buyer is weighing."

[RightPositioning]({{ '/docs/products/right-positioning/' | relative_url }}) surfaces how synthetic buyers in your target segment compare you to live alternatives, and returns the positioning gaps - angles your competitors are not owning that your product can claim. The output is a specific angle, not a general recommendation.

### 3. What to charge

Price is the GTM decision with the widest variance. The same product can price 3-5x differently depending on segment, framing, and packaging. Setting price by looking at competitors or picking a round number that feels right skips the only test that matters: does your target buyer accept this price, and at what point does it become a barrier? Van Westendorp price sensitivity analysis answers that with four questions mapped to your specific segment.

[RightPrice]({{ '/docs/products/right-price/' | relative_url }}) runs Van Westendorp against synthetic buyers matched to your segment and returns an optimal price range, a confidence score from 0 to 90, and a trial strategy recommendation based on how your segment responds to the lower bound of the range.

### 4. What to say

Founders write copy in product language. Buyers think in problem language. The gap between the two shows up as bounce rate after you've paid for traffic. Before publishing, your above-the-fold copy needs to pass one test: can a cold reader who matches your ICP - someone who has never heard of you - state in their own words what the product does, who it's for, and what they'd do next?

[RightMessaging]({{ '/docs/products/right-messaging/' | relative_url }}) runs your landing page or website copy through synthetic buyers in your target segment and returns a conversion likelihood score, a clarity score, and a CTA effectiveness score - one number per element so you know exactly what to fix first.

### 5. How to reach buyers cold

A burned outreach list is gone. You get one first contact with each prospect, and subject lines that feel like spam, openers that start with "I," and CTAs that create friction are invisible to the sender but obvious to the recipient. The validation you need happens before you send - not after you've measured a 2% reply rate across 500 prospects and can't tell if the list was wrong or the copy was wrong.

[RightEngagement]({{ '/docs/products/right-engagement/' | relative_url }}) simulates your cold email and outreach sequences against synthetic recipients who match your prospect profile, returning a reply intent score and a drop-off analysis that identifies where in the sequence buyers disengage.

### 6. Where to show up

Channel selection is usually based on what the founder knows, not where the buyer pays attention. Spending 90 days building content for a channel your ICP doesn't use is a recoverable mistake - but only if you catch it before you've built the whole distribution infrastructure. The right test is to confirm which channels your ICP uses to discover and evaluate tools like yours before you commit to any of them.

[RightChannel]({{ '/docs/products/right-channel/' | relative_url }}) ranks marketing channels by where your ICP pays attention and returns an effort-to-return matrix that shows which channels give you the most signal for the least spend at your current stage.

### 7. Whether paid creative works

Ad creative testing is the validation most founders skip because it feels like it requires production assets and live spend to test. It doesn't. The hook, the visual framing, and the CTA are the three elements most correlated with ad performance, and all three can be simulated before a single dollar of production budget is committed.

[RightAd]({{ '/docs/products/right-ad/' | relative_url }}) tests your ad creative concepts for hook strength, click intent, and audience-creative fit, returning scored results across variations so you build the one most likely to perform - not the one that looked best in the brief.

## Running them in order

These 7 decisions are not independent. Audience determines which price is right, which copy will land, and which channel makes sense. Positioning shapes what your copy can credibly say. Price affects which trial strategy your outreach sequence should use. Running them in order - audience first, then positioning, then price, then copy, then outreach, channel, and creative - means each simulation is calibrated against the output of the one before it.
